Values we see in Egleston Hall

Fiscal Restraint

  • Price tag to develop is astronomical and seems unlikely a modern-day Thomas Egleston will emerge
  • Redeveloping Tate Hall/the southwest corner did not pencil out in 2025 and it's a huge risk to assume that they will
  • All Saints has a history of cutting corners on building projects (eg Ellis and Pritchett) which has exacerbated deferred maintenance on those buildings
  • Demolition and construction would take years and could accelerate the downward trend in attendance and giving (adjusted for inflation) by disrupting the routines of congregants
  • Offices can be improved and accessibility achieved in a more cost-effective way than tearing down the building
  • We have the money in hand to fix the recent damage
  • The greenest building is also the one you already have (per World Green Building Council)

Aesthetics

  • Egleston was at the bottom of the list of buildings the parish should consider developing per a 2024 survey
  • Egleston Hall embodies a quiet dignity befitting its purpose and its congregation
  • Appropriate scale and materials and relative size to the church - a bigger building would potentially read as a generic nonprofit office building as a part of a unified appropriately-scaled church ensemble
  • Redevelopment risks losing cloister connection and cohesion with the church and courtyard

History

  • Civil Rights progress has been made in Egleston under the leadership of legends like Frank Ross, Elbert Tuttle and Ralph McGill on topics like race and later with Harry Pritchett and Geoffrey Hoare on equality for women in the church and on LGBTQ+ issues including responding to the AIDS epidemic
